Fourier Bloom

A small transformer learns modular division by growing a 3D Fourier knot — and the knot is the algorithm.

Nikolay Yudin · May 2026 · Interactive research note

Train a tiny transformer to compute c = a · b−1 mod 97 and something quietly remarkable happens. The network doesn't memorize a 97 × 97 lookup table, even though it could. Instead, it grows a precise geometric object inside itself — a Fourier knot in three dimensions — and reads answers off that object. The knot is not a description of the algorithm; the knot is the algorithm.

This note shows that bloom directly, with two interactive scenes you can scrub through. The first watches the knot form during training, from random noise to a crystalline curve. The second shows what that knot is good for: steering the network's prediction by walking along the curve, rather than nudging it sideways.

The setup is small enough to be honest. A two-block transformer with a 384-dimensional residual stream, trained on division modulo 97. The 96 invertible remainders are the only objects in the world the network needs to understand. The visualizations project the network's unembedding matrix W_U — its final read-out — onto three of its own learned Fourier directions. There is no rotation chosen for effect, no t-SNE, no PCA: every axis is a real column-projection of W_U.

The bloom

Press play. The first few hundred steps are noise — a uniform fog of points around the origin. Then two Fourier modes ignite. They are not the same modes across seeds in detail, but they play the same role: two integer harmonics K₁ and K₂ that together turn the 96 invertible remainders into beads on a Lissajous curve. The classes leave the origin together, spread outward, and lock into a closed knot whose shape never changes again once training settles.

The control on the right tells the same story negatively. W_E, the token embedding, sees exactly the same training signal, but no Fourier role to play — and stays at its random initialization. Structure forms only where the task needs it.

Fig. 1. The 96 invertible remainders self-organize during training. The large panel is W_U projected onto three Fourier directions; the small panel on the right is W_E in the same basis. Scrub the timeline to watch the bloom unfold.

The algorithm lives in many places at once

The knot in W_U is not the whole story. The same Fourier circles bloom simultaneously in every part of the network whose job touches the algorithm — and conspicuously stay absent everywhere else.

This matters because it tells us the bloom is not a quirk of one weight matrix. It is the discrete signature of a single coherent computation distributed across the network's feedforward layers, attention values, and unembedding — all bound to the same Fourier basis.

The same algorithm, built by hand

Because the algorithm is so geometric and so explicit, we can write it ourselves. Construct the embedding so that each token carries the discrete logarithm of its remainder as a Fourier pair (cos K·l, sin K·l). Set the attention so that values from positions a and b arrive at the prediction slot. Set the FFN1 neurons to compute the products that turn (cos K·la, sin K·la) · (cos K·lb, sin K·lb) into cos K·(la − lb). Set W_U to read those Fourier coordinates into per-class logits.

No training. Just the algorithm, written into the weights.

The hand-built network solves the task at machine precision. This is the closest a mechanistic interpretation can come to a proof: if the components we identified in the trained model are enough to compute the task when written explicitly, then those components are not a description of the algorithm. They are sufficient for it. The trained network and the hand-built one are the same algorithm in two presentations — one noisy and redundant, one clean and minimal.

Steering by walking the knot

If the algorithm is the knot, then changing the network's prediction is a geometric operation. Want to push prediction from class c to class c · gΔ? Walk Δ beads along the knot. Each bead is a real class. The path stays on the curve. The network follows.

The conventional approach — what activation-steering papers usually do — is to add a vector to the hidden state that points from the source class to the target. On a curve, that vector is a chord through the knot's interior. The interior is the empty middle of the cube, where no class lives. For small Δ the chord stays close to the curve and the steering mostly works. For Δ approaching 48 (the Nyquist), the chord cuts the cube along its full diameter, passing through a region of the residual stream the model has no idea how to read.

The second scene lets you pick a Δ and watch the two paths side by side: the manifold walk in colour, the linear chord as a faint line. The chord becomes obviously wrong as Δ grows.

Fig. 3. Pick a source class and a shift Δ. The orange arc is the manifold walk — every bead is a real class. The chord cutting across the cube is what a linear steering vector would do.

The price of cutting across the empty middle is measurable.

Dictation — replace hidden state with the manifold point at target Δ 100%
Walking — rotate hidden state along the K-mode Fourier circles by 2π·K·Δ/96 96.7%
Linear chord — add the difference vector once, scaled to Δ ≈ 50%

Dictation is the cleanest control: it puts the network's internal state directly onto the target manifold point and asks the read-out head what class it sees. The head reports the right one every time. Walking is the softer version that keeps everything else about the hidden state intact and only adjusts the Fourier phases; it works on every requested Δ on every one of ten independently trained seeds, with a mean hit rate of 96.7%. Linear chord works half the time at best — and there is nothing to fix about its implementation. The direction in residual space that takes one class to another is curved, not straight, and a chord is the wrong object.

What the knot means

The bloom and the steering are two views of the same fact. A neural network solving a problem with mathematical structure does not learn a function and then store it. It builds a geometric attractor whose shape is the function. Once the attractor exists, every prediction is a position on it; every intervention is a motion across it; every control we care about — accuracy, steering, interpretation — is geometry.

For a task as small as modular division, the attractor is a 3D Fourier knot we can render and fly through. For larger tasks the attractors are higher-dimensional and uglier, but the picture is the same one. Find the manifold and you have found the algorithm. Walk the manifold and you are doing the only intervention the model knows how to follow.
