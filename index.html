Fourier Bloom

A small transformer learns modular division by growing a 3D Fourier knot inside itself.

Nikolay Yudin · May 2026 · Interactive research note

Train a tiny transformer to compute c = a · b−1 mod 97 and you can watch how it learns. The network has enough parameters to memorize a 97 × 97 lookup table, but it doesn't. What it does instead is grow a Fourier knot in three dimensions and read answers off that knot. The knot turns out to be the whole algorithm.

Below are two interactive visualizations. The first lets you scrub through training and watch the knot form from random noise. The second uses the knot to steer the network's predictions, by walking along the curve rather than pushing sideways through it.

The setup is small. Two transformer blocks, residual dimension 384, trained to predict c = a · b−1 mod 97. There are 96 invertible remainders mod 97, and those are the only outputs the network ever produces. The visualizations project the unembedding matrix W_U (the layer that turns hidden states into logits) onto three of its own Fourier directions. No t-SNE, no PCA, no axis chosen for effect: each axis is a column-projection of W_U onto a real cosine or sine basis vector.

The bloom

Press play. The first few hundred steps look like noise: a fog of points around the origin. Then two Fourier modes take over. The exact two harmonics vary across seeds, but the role they play is the same in every run. They are integer harmonics K₁ and K₂, and together they map the 96 invertible remainders onto beads of a Lissajous curve. The 96 points drift away from the origin, spread out, and settle into a knot whose shape is stable for the rest of training.

The small panel on the right shows W_E, the token embedding. It sees the same gradient signal as W_U, but the algorithm gives it no Fourier role to play, and it stays close to its random initialization throughout training. Structure forms where the task uses it and not elsewhere.

Fig. 1. The 96 invertible remainders self-organize during training. The large panel is W_U projected onto three Fourier directions; the small panel on the right is W_E in the same basis. Scrub the timeline to watch the bloom unfold.

The algorithm lives in many places at once

W_U is not the only place this happens. The same Fourier circles appear in every part of the network that participates in the computation, and they do not appear in any part that doesn't.

The bloom is not local to one weight matrix. The same Fourier basis is shared across the feedforward layers, the attention values, and the unembedding, and the same K-mode structure shows up in all of them. The algorithm is a property of that shared basis, not of any single component.

The same algorithm, built by hand

Once the algorithm is this explicit, the next thing to try is to write it by hand. The recipe has four steps. The embedding carries each token's discrete logarithm as a Fourier pair (cos K·l, sin K·l). The attention routes the values from positions a and b into the prediction slot. The FFN1 multiplies the two Fourier pairs into cos K·(la − lb). And W_U reads those coordinates out as per-class logits.

There is no training step. Every weight is set by hand from the recipe above.

The hand-built network solves the task at machine precision. This is about as close to a constructive proof as mechanistic interpretation gets: if you can hand-assemble a working model from the components identified in the trained one, those components are not just a hypothesis about how the trained model works — they are enough to run the task on their own. The trained model and the hand-built model are doing the same computation in two different presentations: one noisy and over-parameterized, the other minimal.

Steering by walking the knot

If the algorithm lives on the knot, changing the network's prediction is a geometric move. To shift the prediction from class c to class c · gΔ, you can walk Δ beads along the curve. Every position on that walk corresponds to a real class that the model already represents, and the head reads the correct logit at each one.

The standard alternative, used in most activation-steering papers, is to add a single difference vector to the hidden state: target class minus source class. On a curve, that vector is a chord through the knot's interior. The interior of the cube contains no classes; it is the region the model never produces during normal operation. For small Δ the chord stays close to the curve and the steering still works most of the time. For Δ approaching 48 (the Nyquist), the chord cuts straight across the cube and lands in a region of the residual stream the model has no representation for.

The second visualization lets you pick a Δ and compare the two paths. The manifold walk is the coloured arc; the linear chord is the faint straight line. As you increase Δ, the chord pulls visibly further away from the curve.

Fig. 3. Pick a source class and a shift Δ. The orange arc is the manifold walk — every bead is a real class. The chord cutting across the cube is what a linear steering vector would do.

The cost of routing through the interior shows up in the hit rates.

Dictation: replace hidden state with the manifold point at target Δ 100%
Walking: rotate hidden state along the K-mode Fourier circles by 2π·K·Δ/96 96.7%
Linear chord: add the difference vector once, scaled to Δ ≈ 50%

Dictation overwrites the hidden state with the manifold point at the target class and asks the head to read it. The head reads the right class on every input. Walking is the gentler version: it keeps the hidden state mostly intact and only rotates the Fourier phases by 2π·K·Δ/96. It hits the requested class on every Δ across all ten seeds, with a mean hit rate of 96.7%. The linear chord caps out around 50%, and there is no obvious tuning that fixes it; the curve the model uses to represent class shifts is not a straight line, so a chord is the wrong object to push along it.

What the knot means

The bloom and the steering point at the same thing. When a network solves a problem with this much structure, it does not learn a function and then store it somewhere; it grows a geometric object whose shape encodes the function. After that, every prediction is a position on the object, every intervention is a motion across it, and questions about accuracy, steering, and interpretation become questions about geometry.

For modular division the attractor is a 3D Fourier knot, small enough to render and fly through. For larger tasks the manifolds will be higher-dimensional and harder to picture, but the underlying claim is the same: finding the manifold means finding the algorithm, and the only interventions that work cleanly are the ones the manifold permits.
